PICU Basics

When Does a Child Need PICU?

Children are shifted to a Pediatric Intensive Care Unit when they have a life-threatening illness or are at high risk of sudden deterioration. Warning signs include fast or difficult breathing, poor responsiveness, persistent low blood pressure, seizures, altered sensorium or the need for strong life-support medicines. In such situations, close monitoring, rapid lab access and the ability to give advanced support (ventilators, inotropes, dialysis) can significantly improve survival.

PICU Transfer

What Parents Should Know Before ICU Transfer

Being told that your child needs PICU is frightening. Before transfer, doctors stabilise the child, secure IV lines and airway if needed, and communicate with the receiving PICU. During transfer, monitoring continues so that any change is detected early. In the PICU, you will see multiple monitors, pumps and machines ‚Äì they help us watch vital signs second-by-second. Parents are encouraged to ask questions and clarify doubts; an informed parent is an important partner in the child‚Äôs care.

Sepsis

Sepsis in Children: Early Warning Signs

Sepsis is a severe infection that affects the whole body and can quickly progress to shock and organ failure. Red flag signs include persistent high or very low temperature, unusually fast breathing, cold hands and feet, mottled or very pale skin, reduced urine, extreme sleepiness or irritability, and poor feeding. Early recognition, prompt antibiotics and fluid resuscitation, and timely PICU admission are key to improving outcomes.

Recognising sepsis early can be life-saving ‚Äì do not delay seeking care.
